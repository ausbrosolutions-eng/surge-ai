# Discovery Call Framework
## The Surge SPIN/PACI Playbook for Restoration Contractor Discovery

**Call length:** 25-30 minutes scheduled, 45 minutes blocked.
**Outcome goal:** Booked Ops Audit ($3,500) OR specific next step with specific date.
**Mindset:** 70% listening, 30% talking. Your job is to uncover pain, not pitch.

---

## Pre-Call Preparation (5-10 Minutes)

### Research Sprint
- [ ] Company website: what do they emphasize? Services, service area, certifications, years in business
- [ ] LinkedIn (owner + 1-2 key staff): tenure, posts, growth content
- [ ] Google Reviews: rating, volume, recent complaints, competitor mentions
- [ ] Indeed/LinkedIn Jobs: current hiring
- [ ] BBB: any accreditation, complaints
- [ ] IICRC: are they certified? Which categories?

### Pre-Call Notes (Populate Before Call)
- Company size (revenue estimate, employee count, truck count)
- Current CRM (if publicly known)
- Market disaster profile (flood, fire, hurricane exposure)
- 1 specific observation to lead with
- 2-3 hypothesis pain points based on size/market
- Target next step based on fit signals

## The Call Structure

### Opening (First 2 Minutes)

**Purpose:** Set frame. Signal you're not going to pitch. Establish pace.

**Script:**
"Hey [First Name], thanks for the time. Before we dig in, let me tell you how I'd like to run this. I want to spend most of the call understanding how [Company] actually runs today, specifically on the claims and collections side. Then in the last 10 minutes we'll talk about whether our 2-week Ops Audit makes sense for you. If it does, we'll book it. If it doesn't, I'll tell you that honestly and we'll both save time. Sound good?"

**Why it works:**
- Signals structure (they relax)
- Eliminates fear of being sold to
- Establishes mutual respect (we'll both save time)

---

### Section 1: Situation Questions (5 Minutes)

**Purpose:** Establish facts. Minimize these - do your research beforehand.

**Questions to ask:**
1. "Roughly how many jobs does [Company] run per month?"
2. "What's your typical average job value? Ballpark is fine."
3. "Water/fire/mold mix - what's the rough breakdown?"
4. "What CRM are you on?"
5. "Who on your team handles the claims side end-to-end? One person, multiple people, or does it move through a pipeline?"
6. "Are you mostly insurance work or mix of insurance and direct-pay?"

**What you're really listening for:**
- Size (informs pricing, audit complexity)
- Insurance mix (80%+ insurance = high claims workflow pain)
- CRM (informs path recommendation)
- Ops ownership (single point of failure OR chaotic)

---

### Section 2: Problem Questions (10-12 Minutes - The Meat)

**Purpose:** Surface frustrations. Get them to say the problem out loud.

**Framework:** Ask one question. Listen fully. Follow with either a deeper probe or the next question. Do NOT pivot to solution-talk during this section.

**Opening pain-discovery question:**
"Walk me through what happens from the moment a homeowner has a loss to the moment you get final payment. Where does that process break down the most?"

*Let them talk. This will surface 3-5 pain points on its own. Write them all down.*

**Follow-up probes (ask 4-5 of these based on what surfaced):**

**On collections:**
- "How many invoices would you estimate you have sitting in 'sent/waiting' right now that are over 60 days old?"
- "What's your process for following up on aging invoices?"
- "Has anyone on the team ever said 'we should really do something about those old invoices'? What happened?"

**On documentation:**
- "When a job gets to billing stage, how often is it blocked because documentation is missing?"
- "Who usually has to chase down missing documents? How long does that take per job?"
- "Ever had a supplement denied because of a documentation gap? What was the dollar impact?"

**On adjuster workflow:**
- "Which carriers give you the most trouble? Which are smoothest?"
- "How do you track which adjusters are slow or unresponsive?"
- "When an adjuster ghosts you on a supplement request, what's your playbook?"

**On lead intake:**
- "How do leads flow from first contact into your CRM? Who does the data entry?"
- "Does that process ever break down? Leads lost, duplicated, mis-assigned?"

**On reporting:**
- "What numbers do you look at weekly to understand how the business is doing? How do you get them?"
- "Are you able to see close rate by rep, revenue by job type, or average days to collect?"

**On team bandwidth:**
- "Who on the team is most overloaded right now? What are they spending their time on?"
- "If [that person] took a week off, what would break?"

---

### Section 3: Implication Questions (5-7 Minutes)

**Purpose:** Take the stated problems and explore downstream consequences. Make the cost concrete. The prospect calculates the damage themselves.

**Framework:** Take what they said in Section 2 and ask "what does that cost you?" Multiple angles.

**If they admitted stuck collections:**
- "If you've got [their number] sitting in collections over 60 days old, and restoration collection recovery rates drop roughly 10% per 30 days of age past 90 days - what does that unrecovered pool look like by year-end if nobody systematically works it?"
- "On a job-by-job basis, has anyone calculated what you're writing off per year? Or is it an unknown number?"

**If they admitted documentation gaps:**
- "When a supplement gets denied because of documentation, how much revenue does that typically cost per job?"
- "If that's happening on [X%] of jobs, and your average supplement is [Y], what's the annual cost of those lost supplements?"

**If they admitted admin bottlenecks:**
- "If [Silvia equivalent] is spending 15-20 hours a week on manual data entry, that's almost half a full-time employee's capacity. What else would you do with that capacity if it was freed up?"
- "Has this ever cost you a customer? Someone who didn't get timely responses because the team was buried?"

**If they admitted no real reporting:**
- "When you don't have data on close rate by rep or revenue by job type, what decisions are you making with your gut that you'd rather make with data?"
- "Ever hired somebody and realized 6 months later the metrics on them weren't what you thought? How much did that cost?"

---

### Section 4: Need-Payoff Questions (3-4 Minutes)

**Purpose:** Get the prospect to describe the value of solving the problem in their own words. Forward-looking. Positive.

**Framework:** "If we could... what would it mean for you?"

**Examples:**
- "If we could recover 15-20% of what's sitting in your collections queue right now, how would that change your Q3?"
- "If your techs were capturing documentation in a way that made supplements auto-approve instead of getting denied, what would that mean for your margin on the average job?"
- "If [admin bottleneck person] had 20 more hours a week available, what would you put them on?"
- "If you could walk in every Monday morning and see exactly what your pipeline, close rate, and collection velocity looked like on one screen, how would that change how you run the business?"

**Critical:** Let them finish. Do not fill silence. Their answer is 10x more persuasive than yours.

---

### Section 5: The Transition to the Audit (5 Minutes)

**Purpose:** Translate what they just told you into the specific reason the Ops Audit is the right next step.

**Script:**
"So let me play back what I heard, and you tell me if I'm close.

[Recap 3-4 specific pains they named. Use their words when possible.]

If we're lining those up honestly, conservative math says you've got somewhere between $[X] and $[Y] in recoverable revenue sitting in your existing pipeline right now. That doesn't count any new leads or marketing work. That's just money you've already earned that hasn't found its way to your bank account.

Does that feel about right to you?"

*Wait for them to agree, push back, or refine the number. They will usually confirm or adjust upward.*

"Okay. So the question isn't whether there's a real problem to solve, it's whether the path to solving it starts with understanding the current state in detail.

That's what the Ops Audit is. Two weeks. $3,500. You give us API access to your CRM and 90 minutes of kickoff time. We give you a 20-page report with your exact collections exposure, documentation gap analysis, data integrity score, tech stack recommendation, and 90-day implementation roadmap. If we can't identify at least $150K in recoverable revenue, you get the full $3,500 back.

And here's the honest part: about 40-60% of the restoration contractors who run the audit decide to have us implement the roadmap. The rest take the roadmap and run it themselves or hand it to their team. Either way works for us. We're not trying to trap you into a retainer."

---

### Section 6: The Close (3 Minutes)

**Script (assume positive path):**
"Does that make sense as a next step?"

*Wait for response.*

**If yes:**
"Great. Two ways to handle this. Option 1: I send you a Stripe payment link and a one-page contract right after this call. You sign and pay today, we kickoff next Tuesday. Option 2: I send them by end of day and we book the kickoff for the following week. Which works better for you?"

**If 'I need to think about it':**
Use the battle card response from `docs/surge/05-battle-cards.md`, objection #5.

**If specific concern raised (price, fit, timing):**
Use the relevant battle card response. Acknowledge, reframe, redirect.

**If 'not right now':**
"Totally fair. What changes between now and it being right? Is it a timing thing on your side, a revenue thing, a team thing? I want to make sure I understand whether it's a 'not right now' vs a 'probably never.'"

---

### Closing (Last 2 Minutes)

**Always end with:**
- Specific next step with specific date (even if they didn't buy)
- Your commitment (what you'll send, when)
- Their commitment (what they'll do, when)

**Example (if audit booked):**
"I'll send the Stripe link and contract within 2 hours. Once you've paid, my assistant will send you a Calendly link to book the 90-minute kickoff call and an intake form. Plan on 2 weeks from kickoff to final report delivery. We'll schedule a 60-minute review call after the report lands. Any questions before we wrap?"

**Example (if 'thinking about it'):**
"Here's what I'll do: I'll send a one-page summary of what we covered today with the specific numbers we discussed. Give it a few days, talk to [partner name if mentioned], and let's reconnect next [specific day]. Fair?"

## Mistakes to Avoid (Cherry Anti-Patterns)

1. **Too many Situation questions.** Feels like an interrogation. Cap at 5-6. Do research beforehand.

2. **Skipping to the pitch before pain is surfaced.** If you find yourself talking about the audit before minute 20, you skipped discovery. Re-engage with a Problem question.

3. **Filling silence after Need-Payoff questions.** When you ask "what would that mean for you?" and they pause, do NOT fill the silence. Their answer is your close.

4. **Weak Implication questions.** "So that's a problem?" is not an Implication question. Go deeper: "What does that mean in dollars? What does it mean for your team? What does it mean for your next 12 months?"

5. **Letting the call end without a specific next step.** Every call ends with a date on the calendar. If the prospect won't commit to one, that's a qualification signal (they're not really interested).

6. **Talking more than 40% of the time.** If you're doing most of the talking, you're not discovering. Stop. Ask a question. Wait.

7. **Over-selling after they say yes.** The moment they agree to the audit, shift to logistics. Don't keep selling. You'll talk them out of it.

## Calibration Examples

### A "hot" call looks like:
- They name 3+ specific pains in Section 2 without much probing
- They self-calculate dollar losses in Section 3
- They say "that would be huge" or "that would change everything" in Section 4
- They ask specific questions about the audit process in Section 5
- They commit to a date in Section 6

**Next step:** Book the audit today.

### A "warm" call looks like:
- They name 1-2 pains but push back on magnitude
- They're curious but hedging
- They want to think about it, talk to a partner, or circle back

**Next step:** Send the follow-up email with math. Schedule a 15-min follow-up call in 7-10 days. Multi-thread by asking for the partner's contact.

### A "cold" call looks like:
- They deflect most Problem questions
- They claim everything is fine
- They don't articulate any pain
- They're not the decision-maker

**Next step:** End politely. Disqualify. Add to long-term nurture.

## Practice Routine (Weekly)

- Record every discovery call with prospect permission ("Mind if I record? I do this on all calls so I can review them and make sure I captured everything right for follow-up.")
- Review 1 recorded call per week. Note: what did I do well, what did I miss, where did I talk when I should have listened.
- Role-play with a friend or record yourself running through the framework solo. Watch back. Refine.
- Build a swipe file of best responses and golden quotes from prospects.

The top 10% of AEs at any company aren't smarter than the bottom 50%. They just practice more.
