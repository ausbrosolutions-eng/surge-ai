# Battle Cards
## Objection Handling & Competitive Positioning for Surge Advisory

**Purpose:** Give Austin the specific, data-backed responses to the objections that come up in discovery calls. Never defensive. Acknowledge, reframe, redirect to math.

---

## Objection #1: "We already have a marketing agency"

**What they're really saying:** I'm not looking for another vendor. I have an incumbent I trust or am locked into.

**Response:**
"Totally makes sense. Most restoration contractors we talk to have a marketing agency. Surge isn't a marketing agency. We work on the back half of the business: ops infrastructure, claims workflow, documentation automation, collections recovery. Most marketing agencies drive leads to a business whose pipeline is leaking 15-25% out the back. We plug the leak. We work alongside agencies, not instead of them. Worth 15 minutes to see how they fit together?"

**Why it works:** Reframes Surge out of the "agency" category entirely. Positions as complementary, not competitive.

---

## Objection #2: "We already have a CRM/ops system we're happy with"

**What they're really saying:** Switching costs scare me. I don't want a migration project.

**Response:**
"Good. The goal of our audit isn't to switch your CRM. It's to figure out whether what you have is actually working for you, and if not, whether the answer is better automation on top of it or a migration. We've built on JobNimbus, Albi, Xcelerate, and PSA. All of them can work. What matters is whether your current setup is returning the revenue you're leaving on the table. The audit shows you that number in 14 days. $3,500. Refundable if we can't find $150K. What's the risk in seeing what we'd find?"

**Why it works:** Separates "doing the audit" from "switching tools." Lowers the stakes of the conversation.

---

## Objection #3: "We've tried this kind of thing before and it didn't stick"

**What they're really saying:** I've been burned. Probably by a marketing agency or a generic "automation consultant" who delivered a PDF and disappeared.

**Response:**
"That's the most common story I hear. Most consultants deliver a report and a retainer invoice. The report goes in a drawer. The retainer gets cancelled in 3 months. What we do differently: the audit is a product, not a gateway. If the roadmap makes sense to implement, we either implement it with you (phased retainer) or hand it off to your team cleanly. If it doesn't make sense, you keep the report and we shake hands. No dependency engineered into the relationship. What specifically didn't work the last time? Let me pressure-test whether we'd hit the same wall."

**Why it works:** Acknowledges their pain, differentiates on structure, invites them to talk about the specific failure so you can diagnose it.

---

## Objection #4: "It's too expensive"

**What they're really saying:** I don't see the ROI math yet.

**Never defend on price.** Surface cost of inaction first.

**Response:**
"Before we get to price, help me understand: what do you estimate is currently sitting in your collections queue past 90 days? And what's your close rate on supplements, roughly? No judgment either way, I just want to make sure we're doing math against real numbers for your business."

[After they answer, do the math out loud with them]

"Okay, so if you've got [X] in old collections and you're losing 30-40% of supplement revenue to documentation gaps, conservative math says you're leaving [$Y] on the table per year. The audit is $3,500. If we recover 10% of what we identify, it pays for itself 5-10x in the first 90 days. The retainer after that is $3,500-$6,500/month and projected to recover multiples of that. What's expensive is what you're already losing that nobody's measuring."

**Why it works:** Cherry's principle: the math is your closer. Help them calculate the number themselves.

---

## Objection #5: "I need to think about it / talk to my partner"

**What they're really saying:** I'm not convinced yet but I don't want to say no.

**Response:**
"Totally fair. What's the main thing you'd want to get clear on before making a call? Because if it's a question about how the audit works, I can answer it right now. If it's a question about budget or whether [partner name] is bought in, that's different and I want to respect their involvement. What's actually the sticking point?"

**Why it works:** Separates "I need to think" from specific unresolved concerns. Either uncovers a real objection or confirms they need more stakeholders. Never let "I need to think about it" be the end of the conversation.

**Follow-up if multi-threading needed:**
"Got it. Want me to put together a one-pager so you can share it with [partner name], or would it be easier to set up a quick 15-minute call with both of you? Either works."

---

## Objection #6: "Our customers don't complain about that"

**What they're really saying:** I don't believe the problem is as big as you're saying.

**Response:**
"That's the tricky part. Customers who have a bad experience usually don't complain, they just don't use you again and they tell 2-3 people. On a restoration job, the homeowner typically doesn't know why their claim took 199 days to close. They just know it was frustrating. That's why we use the data, not the customer feedback, to find the gaps. The $1.3M we found at Rehab was sitting there quietly. Nobody was calling to complain about it. It was just aging in their system. Worth running the audit to see if your business has a quiet version of the same?"

**Why it works:** Explains why the absence of complaints isn't evidence of absence of problems. Brings it back to data.

---

## Objection #7: "We don't have time for another project right now"

**What they're really saying:** I'm already stretched. Can't take on more.

**Response:**
"That's actually the strongest argument for running the audit. Almost every restoration business that tells me they're too busy is too busy because their team is doing manual work that software should be doing. Silvia at Rehab was spending 15-20 hrs/week on WhatsApp-to-CRM data entry. Jared was spending 4-6 hrs/week building ZIP files manually. They had no time for the audit. But the audit bought them back dozens of hours per week. The audit itself requires about 2 hours of your time across the 2 weeks. That's it. If finding an extra 20-40 hours per week for your team sounds worth 2 hours of your time, the math is obvious."

**Why it works:** Reframes "too busy" from an objection to a qualifying signal. Addresses actual time cost.

---

## Objection #8: "Do you have experience with our specific software?"

**What they're really saying:** I don't want to be a learning curve.

**Response (if yes):**
"Yes. We've audited restoration contractors on JobNimbus (our primary client is on it), and we've scoped builds on Albi, Xcelerate, and PSA. Our methodology is tool-agnostic because the problems are consistent across CRMs. What specifically do you use?"

**Response (if no):**
"Honest answer: we haven't audited a contractor on [specific tool] yet. But every restoration CRM has the same three problems - collections bleeding, documentation gaps, and data blindness. The way you surface them is the same regardless of tool. I'd rather tell you that up front than pretend otherwise. If the audit doesn't find real value, you get the refund. That's the guarantee."

**Why it works:** Honesty earns trust. Admitting gaps where you have them is more convincing than false expertise.

---

## Objection #9: "How do we know the audit is worth $3,500?"

**What they're really saying:** I've been burned by consulting fees before.

**Response:**
"Three reasons. First: the deliverable is concrete. You get a 20-page report with your own data, a tech stack evaluation, and a 90-day roadmap. Yours to keep forever, implement yourself or with us. Second: the guarantee. If we can't identify $150K in recoverable revenue, you get the full $3,500 back. In practice we've found $350K-$1.3M on every audit we've run. Third: the audit fee credits against any retainer engagement if you decide to move forward with us. So if you like what you see, the audit was free. If you don't, you get your money back. What's the risk?"

**Why it works:** Triple-safe structure (deliverable + guarantee + credit) removes virtually all downside.

---

## Objection #10: "We're too small for this"

**What they're really saying:** I think this is enterprise stuff, not for a 3-truck operation.

**Response (if they're under $500K):**
"You might actually be right. The audit is overkill for sub-$500K restoration businesses. Not because the problems don't exist, but because the dollars at stake don't justify the fee. I'd probably point you toward a simpler setup: get on JobNimbus, nail required fields, use Encircle in the field, and build a basic collection SMS sequence. Happy to share a 30-minute call and give you that roadmap for free if you're open to it."

**Response (if they're $500K-$1M):**
"You're actually in the sweet spot. Small enough that the gap between current state and good is huge, big enough that the dollars involved justify the fee. Rehab was at your size when we started. We're seeing $300K+ in Year 1 recovery on businesses doing $2M+. The ratio scales down but so does the price of inaction. Worth 15 minutes to see if the math works for your size?"

**Why it works:** Honest disqualification for prospects who genuinely shouldn't buy. Earns respect. And the "free 30-min roadmap for the under-$500K segment" becomes a goodwill pipeline for when they scale.

## Competitive Positioning

### vs. Generic Marketing Agencies (e.g., Scorpion, Hook Agency, Builder Agency)

**What they do well (acknowledge honestly):**
- Google Ads campaigns
- GBP optimization
- Local SEO
- Reviews management
- Content + social

**What they do poorly:**
- Ops infrastructure (don't touch it)
- Claims workflow (no expertise)
- CRM integration (surface-level)
- Revenue recovery from existing pipeline (zero attention)

**Positioning line:**
"Marketing agencies drive leads into your business. Surge makes sure the business can actually handle those leads profitably. Most contractors have the opposite problem of what agencies solve."

### vs. Generic Automation Consultants

**What they do well:**
- Zapier builds
- General workflow automation
- Broad tech stack knowledge

**What they do poorly:**
- Restoration-specific workflows (don't know the difference between 3.3 and 5.0 status)
- Insurance/claims terminology (don't know what a supplement is)
- Industry-specific tools (never heard of Encircle, Xactimate, RIB, OCS)
- Actual implementation accountability (they build and disappear)

**Positioning line:**
"We built the collection sequence knowing that 5.0 means 'Invoice Built/Sent to Collections' and that the average adjuster open caseload is 110-140 claims. Generalist automation consultants build Zaps. We build restoration ops systems."

### vs. Large Consulting Firms (e.g., Deloitte, Accenture, local BizOps consultants)

**What they do well:**
- Enterprise-scale engagements
- Org design
- Change management frameworks
- Senior experience

**What they do poorly:**
- Small-business context (you're below their size threshold)
- Implementation (they advise, don't build)
- Hands-on execution (they billable-hour away and disappear)
- Price (10-20x our engagement cost)

**Positioning line:**
"Deloitte will charge you $150K to tell you what we'll find in 14 days for $3,500. And they won't build the automation afterward. They'll recommend a 'transformation initiative.' We just do the work."

### vs. DIY / In-House (their own team trying to fix it)

**What they do well:**
- Domain knowledge of the business
- Already employed
- Free (sunk cost)

**What they do poorly:**
- Dedicated time (nobody has it)
- Technical depth (they can't build the automations)
- Outside perspective (they've normalized the pain)
- Accountability (no deadline pressure)

**Positioning line:**
"Most restoration owners think they'll 'get to it eventually.' It's been on their list for 2 years. We've seen too many businesses lose $300K+ per year while waiting for 'eventually.' What you're paying for is the deadline, the expertise, and the fact that someone whose only job is to fix it actually sits down and fixes it."

## The "I'll Think About It" Follow-Up Email

Send within 24 hours of any call that ends with "I'll think about it":

**Subject:** Quick recap + one question

Hey [First Name],

Thanks for the time today. Quick recap of what we covered, then one question I forgot to ask:

**Where you are today:**
- [Specific pain point #1]
- [Specific pain point #2]
- [Specific dollar estimate of their exposure]

**What the audit would show you:**
- Exact collections exposure by job and age
- Documentation gap against billing requirements
- Data integrity score
- Tech stack recommendation for your size
- 90-day implementation roadmap

**Cost of inaction:**
Based on what you shared, conservatively $[X]/month in unrecovered revenue.

**The question I forgot to ask:**
If we did run the audit and it landed exactly like I'm describing - a clear $150K+ in recoverable revenue with a roadmap to get it - what would actually happen next? Would you implement it? With us, internally, or a mix?

Want to understand the finish line so we don't waste each other's time.

Austin

**Why it works:** Restates the value, isolates whether they're a qualified prospect, forces them to imagine the end state. Highest-leverage follow-up email in the playbook.
